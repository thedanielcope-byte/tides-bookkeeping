About the Area

Horry County Is
SC's Tourism &
Coastal Economy Hub.

Horry County is South Carolina's tourism capital — and one of the fastest-growing counties in the entire United States. The Grand Strand, stretching 60 miles of Atlantic coastline, draws over 20 million visitors annually and anchors a massive hospitality, entertainment, and real estate economy that creates extraordinary demand for small business financial services.

But Horry County is far more than its beaches. Conway — the county seat — is a historic inland city. Coastal Carolina University has transformed the county's educational and professional landscape. And a construction boom fueled by retiring baby boomers and remote workers relocating from the Northeast has made Horry County one of the most active construction and real estate markets in the Southeast.

History & Economic Identity

Horry County's economic story is one of the most remarkable tourism transformations in American history. Through most of the 20th century, the county was an isolated farming and timber region — connected to the rest of SC by limited roads and the Waccamaw River. The arrival of US-501 and later the development of the Grand Strand as a major tourism destination changed everything.

Today, Horry County is the second-fastest growing county in South Carolina, with tourism, retirement, and remote-worker relocation driving explosive population and business growth. Coastal Carolina University, with 10,000+ students in Conway, has transformed the county's education and professional services landscape. Burroughs & Chapin — the county's largest private employer — has developed millions of square feet of retail, commercial, and residential real estate across the Grand Strand.

The seasonal nature of the tourism economy creates unique bookkeeping challenges for Horry County businesses: dramatic revenue swings from summer peak to off-season, complex vacation rental income tracking, tip payroll management for hundreds of restaurants, and the cash flow planning that's essential for businesses that earn most of their revenue in 3–4 months. That's exactly what Tides specializes in.

Key Corridors & Communities

Kings Highway (US-17)
The Grand Strand's commercial spine — stretching 60 miles from Little River to Pawleys Island, lined with hotels, restaurants, retail, and entertainment businesses.
Broadway at the Beach
Myrtle Beach's premier entertainment complex — restaurants, bars, attractions, and retail generating millions in annual revenue for dozens of small businesses.
Market Common
A mixed-use lifestyle district on the former Myrtle Beach Air Force Base — boutique retail, restaurants, professional offices, and a growing residential community.
Murrells Inlet Marshwalk
A waterfront dining and entertainment corridor famous for fresh seafood — one of SC's most concentrated clusters of restaurant small businesses.
SC-544 / Carolina Forest
The inland residential and retail growth corridor — major shopping centers, medical facilities, and service businesses serving the county's fastest-growing residential area.
Conway / US-501 Inland
The gateway from the inland Midlands to the beach — professional services, healthcare, and the CCU campus anchoring an inland economy separate from tourism.
Little River Waterway
The northern Grand Strand's waterfront community — marinas, waterfront restaurants, charter fishing businesses, and vacation rental properties along the Intracoastal Waterway.
Pawleys Island / Litchfield
The southern Grand Strand's upscale coastal communities — high-end vacation rentals, boutique shops, art galleries, and resort real estate businesses.
