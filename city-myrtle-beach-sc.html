📍 About Myrtle Beach, SC

The City Behind the Business.

Myrtle Beach is far more than a beach vacation destination — it's a complex, multi-layered economy that spans hospitality, real estate, healthcare, higher education, retail, and construction across Horry County's 60-mile Grand Strand coastline.

History & Economic Growth

Myrtle Beach was incorporated in 1938 and spent its early decades as a modest coastal resort town anchored by the original Pavilion amusement park. The construction of US-501 connecting the coast to the SC interior — and postwar prosperity that made car travel accessible — transformed it into the mass-market tourism destination it is today. By the 1970s and '80s, the Grand Strand had become one of America's most popular vacation destinations, drawing millions from the Southeast and Mid-Atlantic.

The economic story of the past two decades has been equally remarkable: Myrtle Beach has attracted permanent residents at a dramatic pace, with Horry County consistently ranking among the fastest-growing counties in the country. Coastal Carolina University in Conway has become a significant economic anchor, and the healthcare system (Grand Strand Medical Center, Conway Medical Center, Tidelands Health) has expanded dramatically to serve the growing year-round population.

Today, Myrtle Beach's economy is a genuine dual-track: the massive seasonal tourism economy and a growing year-round residential and services economy that increasingly operates independent of beach season.

Grand Strand Communities

Broadway at the Beach
The anchor of Myrtle Beach's entertainment district — 350 acres of restaurants, shops, attractions, and nightlife drawing millions of visitors annually.
Market Common
Upscale mixed-use development on the former Myrtle Beach Air Force Base — boutique retail, restaurants, a movie theater, and a growing professional services community.
North Myrtle Beach
Separate city to the north with its own commercial corridor — famous for the Shag music scene and Ocean Drive, with strong local retail and hospitality businesses.
Murrells Inlet
The "Seafood Capital of South Carolina" — the Marshwalk's famous restaurant row and a thriving community of fishing, boating, and coastal service businesses.
Conway
Horry County seat 13 miles inland — home to Coastal Carolina University, a growing downtown district, and professional services serving the entire county.
Pawleys Island / Litchfield
Upscale southern Grand Strand communities with a quieter resort character, strong real estate activity, and professional services catering to second-home owners.

Inland & Growing Areas

Carolina Forest
One of SC's fastest-growing planned communities — massive residential development with retail, medical offices, and professional services expanding rapidly.
Socastee / Forestbrook
Inland communities with established neighborhoods, commercial corridors, and a strong base of year-round residential businesses west of the beach strip.

Major Roads & Routes

🛣️
US-17 / Kings Highway (Ocean Blvd Corridor)
The historic coastal highway running the full length of the Grand Strand — the primary commercial artery connecting every beach community from Little River to Pawleys Island.
🛣️
US-501 (Primary Gateway)
The main highway connecting Myrtle Beach to I-95 and the SC interior — dense with commercial development from Conway to the beach including Broadway at the Beach and major retail corridors.
🛣️
SC-544 / Farrow Pkwy
East-west connector through Carolina Forest and the Market Common district — increasingly important commercial corridor as the inland communities grow.
🛣️
Robert Grissom Pkwy / 21st Ave N Corridor
Myrtle Beach's main inland bypass — connecting Broadway at the Beach northward through the medical district, entertainment venues, and major retail centers.
🛣️
SC-31 / Carolina Bays Pkwy
The northern bypass connecting North Myrtle Beach to the Conway corridor — critical for freight and commuters in the growing northern Grand Strand area.
