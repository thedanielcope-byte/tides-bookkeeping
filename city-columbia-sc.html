📍 About Columbia, SC

The City Behind the Business.

Columbia is more than the state capital — it's a city with deep roots, a major university, a large military presence, and a growing creative economy that shapes the small businesses we serve.

History & Economic Growth

Columbia was established in 1786 as South Carolina's purpose-built capital city — one of the first planned cities in the United States. Its central location in the state was deliberately chosen to serve all SC residents equally. The city survived the Civil War's devastation (General Sherman's troops burned much of the city in 1865) and rebuilt to become the commercial and governmental center of the state.

The University of South Carolina, founded in 1801, has been a cornerstone of the city's economic identity for over two centuries. Fort Jackson — the largest and most active Army training base in the United States — adds tens of thousands of military personnel and civilian employees to the regional economy.

Today, Columbia's economy is diversified across government, healthcare, higher education, finance, and a growing technology corridor anchored by the USC Research Park. The Vista arts district and Five Points entertainment area anchor a vibrant small business culture in the heart of the city.

Downtown & Urban Neighborhoods

The Vista
Columbia's premier arts, dining, and entertainment district along the Congaree River — home to galleries, upscale restaurants, and creative businesses.
Five Points
USC's entertainment hub — a dense mix of restaurants, bars, retail shops, and service businesses catering to students and young professionals.
Main Street / Downtown
The government and financial core — State House grounds, law firms, banks, insurance offices, and a growing mixed-use commercial corridor.
Shandon
Historic residential neighborhood adjacent to Five Points with boutique shops, coffee houses, and professional services along Devine Street.
Forest Acres
Established east-side enclave with dense retail corridors, medical offices, and a strong base of professional service businesses.
Cayce & West Columbia
Across the river and rapidly developing — State Street retail, breweries, and a growing small business ecosystem on the Lexington County side.

Greater Columbia & Suburbs

Lexington
Fast-growing suburb west of Columbia — one of the state's most active retail and residential growth zones with thousands of small businesses.
Irmo
Northwest Columbia suburb with strong retail, professional services, and access to Lake Murray — a major recreational and residential draw.
Harbison
Major retail and commercial corridor in northwest Columbia — dense with franchise and independent businesses serving a large residential population.
Blythewood / Elgin
Growing northern suburbs with new residential development, increasing commercial activity, and expanding small business communities.

Major Roads & Interstates

🛣️
I-26 (East-West)
The primary east-west interstate through Columbia, connecting Charleston to the coast with Greenville and the Upstate — Columbia's main freight and commuter corridor.
🛣️
I-20 (Beltline / East-West)
Circles the southern and eastern perimeter of Columbia, connecting Augusta to Florence. The Beltline section is a major commercial corridor with retail, medical, and light industrial businesses.
🛣️
I-77 (North-South)
Runs north from downtown Columbia toward Charlotte — connects growing suburbs like Blythewood and Rock Hill to the city's commercial core.
🛣️
Garners Ferry Road / US-76
Southeast corridor with dense commercial development — restaurants, car dealerships, medical offices, and service businesses serving Fort Jackson communities.
🛣️
Two Notch Road / US-1
Northeast commercial spine through northeast Columbia — major retail strip connecting downtown to Fort Jackson and the northeast suburbs.
